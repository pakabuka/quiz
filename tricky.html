MPRE Conflict of Interest Quiz

Focus: current-client vs. former-client conflicts, direct adversity, substantial relationship, consentability, and who must consent.

Quick approach: Ask: (1) current or former client? (2) same matter / same proceeding / substantially related? (3) directly adverse, materially adverse, or only economically adverse? (4) if conflict exists, is it waivable and whose informed written consent is needed?
Answered: 0 / 12

Question 1
Lawyer represents Paula in a contract action against David. David asks Lawyer to represent him in the same action because he believes Lawyer is especially skilled and both clients are willing to consent. May Lawyer represent both Paula and David?
Question 2
Lawyer represents Defendant Corp in unrelated corporate matters. Plaintiff asks Lawyer to sue Defendant Corp in a separate negligence action. Defendant Corp is a current client of Lawyer, but Lawyer does not represent Plaintiff yet. Which is most accurate?
Question 3
Lawyer represents two current clients, each of whom owns a different software company. The companies compete in the marketplace, but Lawyer handles unrelated tax work for one and lease negotiations for the other. Neither client is suing or negotiating against the other. Is Lawyer subject to discipline for representing both without consent?
Question 4
Lawyer formerly represented Frank in negotiating the sale of a small medical practice. Now Client Carla asks Lawyer to represent her in suing Frank over alleged fraud in that same sale transaction. Carla is not otherwise adverse to any current client of Lawyer. May Lawyer represent Carla?
Question 5
Lawyer formerly represented Fiona in drafting a commercial lease five years ago. Lawyer now wants to represent Current Client Carl in a personal-injury action against Fiona arising from a car accident. Which is the best answer?
Question 6
Lawyer formerly represented Former Client F in a regulatory investigation. Lawyer now represents Current Client C in a separate transaction that is not materially adverse to F. However, Lawyer knows confidential facts from the prior representation that could be useful in advising C. What is the best answer?
Question 7
At Lawyer’s old firm, Lawyer personally worked for Former Client F on a patent-licensing dispute and learned material confidential information. After joining a new firm, Lawyer is asked to represent Client C against F in the same dispute. Which is most accurate?
Question 8
Lawyer represents Buyer and Seller in unrelated matters. Buyer asks Lawyer to bring a claim against Seller in a new lawsuit. The claim is unrelated to Lawyer’s existing work for either client. Both clients want to waive the conflict. May Lawyer proceed?
Question 9
Lawyer already represents Paula as plaintiff and David as defendant in the same lawsuit. Both Paula and David want Lawyer to continue representing them, and both give informed consent, confirmed in writing. Is Lawyer subject to discipline if Lawyer continues to represent both sides in that lawsuit?
Question 10
Lawyer formerly represented Former Client F in a merger negotiation. Lawyer now wants to represent Client C in a matter that is substantially related to that merger work, but C’s interests are aligned with F rather than adverse to F. Which is the best answer?
Question 11
Lawyer represents Current Client A in lease negotiations. Lawyer also represents Current Client B in drafting employee handbooks. A asks Lawyer to sue B in a separate tort action. Lawyer reasonably believes she can competently and diligently represent both, and the representation is not otherwise prohibited by law. What else is required if Lawyer wishes to proceed?
Question 12
Lawyer represented Former Client F in drafting a confidentiality agreement. Years later, Lawyer wants to represent Client C against F in a dispute over that same confidentiality agreement. C gives informed written consent, but F refuses. May Lawyer represent C?
